# Message Templates

A copy library for every stage of LinkedIn outreach. Each template is a starting point, not a script — always swap the placeholders for something true and specific to the person. Placeholders look like `{Name}`, `{their post topic}`, `{shared thing}`, `{specific project}`, `{role}`, `{company}`.

**Length rules:** connection-request notes must fit **300 characters**. DMs and InMail have more room but stay tight and mobile-scannable. When in doubt, cut a sentence.

**Table of contents**
1. Connection requests (≤300 chars)
2. Opening DMs (after they connect)
3. Cold InMail (not yet connected)
4. The ask for a warm introduction
5. Follow-ups when there's no response
6. Thank-you notes (after a conversation)

8. Re-engagement (a contact that went cold)

## 1. Connection requests (≤300 characters)

The note attached to a connection request. One sharp, personalized line beats a paragraph. Lead with the hook, close with a light reason to connect.

**a) Admired their work / recent post**
> Hi {Name} — your post on {their post topic} was genuinely useful; {one concrete thing you did or took from it}. I'm {your one-line identity} and would love to connect and follow your work.

*Why it works:* proves you actually engaged with them, gives before it asks, and the "ask" is just to connect.

**b) Shared connection / mutual**
> Hi {Name} — we're both connected to {mutual}, and I've been meaning to reach out since I'm {your identity} working on {relevant thing}. Would be great to connect.

*Why it works:* borrows trust from the mutual (social value) and states a relevant reason.

**c) Same school / alumni**
> Hi {Name} — fellow {school} grad here. I saw you're now {role} at {company}, which is exactly the kind of work I'm moving toward as {your identity}. Would love to connect.

*Why it works:* the shared background is an instant, honest angle.

**d) Recruiter (role-specific)**
> Hi {Name} — I saw you hire for {function} roles at {company}. I'm a {your role} ({1–2 key skills}, {a credential or project}) actively looking in {location/remote}. Would love to connect and be on your radar for relevant openings.

*Why it works:* tells the recruiter exactly what you are and what you want, fast — their job is matching, so make matching easy.

**e) Peer / analyst at a target company (for insight, not a job)**
> Hi {Name} — I'm an {your role} exploring teams doing {type of work} well, and {company}'s came up. Would love to connect and hear a bit about what it's like on your team sometime.

*Why it works:* low-pressure, peer-to-peer, sets up a future conversation without demanding one now.

**f) Same city / community**
> Hi {Name} — fellow {city} {profession}. I've been following {something they/their company does} and would love to connect with more people in the local {field} scene.

**g) Event follow-up**
> Hi {Name} — great to (hear you at / meet you around) {event}. Your point on {specific thing} stuck with me. Connecting so we can keep in touch.

## 2. Opening DMs (after they connect)

Once they accept, this is the real first message. Still concise. Reference why you connected, add a bit more value or a specific, easy question. Do **not** dump your whole ask in the first line.

**a) After an admired-their-work connect**
> Thanks for connecting, {Name}! I meant it about {their post/work} — {a sentence of specific, genuine follow-up}. Curious: {one specific question about how they did it or where they're taking it}?

**b) Job-search insight ask (peer)**
> Thanks for connecting! I'm trying to get a clear picture of {teams/companies} doing {type of work}, and I'd value your perspective. Would you be open to a quick 15-min chat in the next couple of weeks? Totally understand if now's not a good time.

**c) Recruiter follow-through**
> Thanks for connecting, {Name}. Quick summary in case it's useful: I'm a {role} with {1–2 skills + a strong project or credential}, targeting {role types} in {location/remote}. Happy to send my portfolio/CV — and if anything relevant opens up, I'd love to be considered.

*Why these work:* they respect the person's time, make the next step small and specific, and give an easy out (which paradoxically raises reply rates).

## 3. Cold InMail (not yet connected)

InMail reaches people you're not connected to, so the **first line has to earn the open** — no "I hope this finds you well." Front-load the specific hook.

> {Name} — {specific, true observation about their work/post/company move}. {One line on who you are and the honest reason you're reaching out.} {One small, specific ask or offer.}
>
> Example: "Aditya — the way your team rebuilt onboarding analytics after the SaaS migration is exactly the problem I love working on. I'm a data analyst who's shipped a similar hospital-admissions pipeline end to end. If you're open to it, I'd value 15 minutes to hear how your team approaches it — and I'm happy to share what worked in mine."

*Why it works:* opens on them (not you), offers value both directions, and asks for a small, clear amount of time.

## 4. The ask for a warm introduction

The single highest-leverage move. Use it at the end of a good conversation, or in a message to someone who knows your target. Make the intro easy to make.

**a) End-of-conversation ask (verbal or message)**
> This was genuinely helpful, {Name} — thank you. One thing: is there anyone else you'd be open to introducing me to who's doing {relevant work}? No pressure at all if not.

**b) Referencing prior contacts to reach a senior person**
> Hi {Name} — I've been speaking with {Person A} and {Person B} on your team about {topic}, and they suggested I reach out to you to learn more about {the group / a role}. Would you be open to a short conversation?

*Why (b) works:* it's no longer cold — you arrive with internal context and a referral, which is exactly how you get busy senior people to respond.

**c) Make-it-easy intro request (to a connector)**
> Would you be comfortable introducing me to {Target}? To make it easy, here's a two-line blurb you can forward: "{one sentence on who you are} — {one sentence on the specific, relevant reason for the intro}." Totally fine if not.

*Why it works:* you've done the connector's work for them, so saying yes costs almost nothing.

## 5. Follow-ups when there's no response

Most messages get ignored — that's normal, not personal. One well-timed, gracious nudge is worth sending; endless pinging is not. Space follow-ups ~5–7 days apart, and stop after the second.

**a) First nudge (add value, don't just "bump")**
> Hi {Name} — following up in case this slipped by. Since I last wrote, {a small relevant update, or a genuinely useful link/thought tied to their work}. Still would love to {the original small ask} whenever it suits you.

**b) Second nudge (graceful close, leaves the door open)**
> Hi {Name} — I know things get busy, so I won't keep pinging. If {the ask} is ever relevant down the line, I'd still love to connect. Either way, I'll keep following your work on {topic}.

*Why (b) works:* it's low-ego and warm, which often gets a reply precisely because you removed the pressure.

## 6. Thank-you notes (after a conversation)

Send within 24 hours. Skip the generic "thanks for your time." Name the specific thing you valued and what you'll do with it — this is what makes you memorable and starts building trust.

> {Name}, thank you for the conversation — your point on {specific thing X} genuinely shifted how I'm thinking about {topic}. I'm going to {concrete action you'll take because of it}. I'd love to stay in touch and update you on how it goes.

**If they made an intro or offered help:**
> …And thank you for offering to connect me with {Person} — that means a lot. I'll follow up thoughtfully and keep you posted.

## 7. Nurture touchpoints (keeping the relationship warm)

Relationships deepen through genuine, no-ask touchpoints over time. A few per year per person is plenty. Reliability + consistency + authenticity are the whole game.

**a) Congratulations on a milestone**
> Congrats on {new role / launch / promotion / milestone}, {Name}! Really well deserved given {something specific you know about their work}. Excited to see what you do with it.

**b) Share something relevant (give-first)**
> Hey {Name} — saw {this article / talk / tool} on {topic they care about} and immediately thought of you. {One line on why it's relevant to them.} No reply needed — just thought it might be useful.

**c) Genuine check-in**
> Hi {Name} — it's been a while! How's {a real thing you know they were working on / navigating} going? Would love to hear where things landed.

*Why these work:* they cost the recipient nothing, prove you remember and pay attention, and keep you present without ever asking for something.

---

## 8. Re-engagement (a contact that went cold)

For someone you connected with or spoke to a while ago and lost touch with. Acknowledge the gap lightly, lead with value, no guilt.

> Hi {Name} — it's been too long since {how you met / last spoke}. I've been {a genuine, brief update relevant to them}, and your work on {topic} came to mind. Would love to reconnect properly — and if there's ever a way I can be useful to you, I'm glad to.

*Why it works:* it reopens the door with warmth and a give, not a cold "hey remember me, I need something."

---

## Universal rules for every template

- **Research first.** The placeholder `{their post topic}` should be a real thing you read, not a guess.
- **One clear ask, max.** Two asks in one message usually gets zero.
- **Give the person an easy out** ("no pressure," "totally fine if not"). It reliably *raises* response rates.
- **Cut ruthlessly.** If a sentence isn't earning its place, delete it — especially in a 300-char note.
- **Never sound automated.** If you could send it verbatim to 100 people, it's too generic. Fix the hook.
