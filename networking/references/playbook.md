# Networking Playbook (Strategy Layer)

The strategy behind the messages: who to target, how to research them, how to sequence outreach, how to convert conversations into warm intros, how to nurture relationships so they compound, and how to build your own value in parallel. Read this for any planning, targeting, or strategy request (Modes A and C), and for the nurturing system (Mode D).

## 1. The value framework (with LinkedIn examples)

Every relationship is built on some value the other person perceives. Before reaching out, decide which type is your honest angle:

- **Financial** — "I can help you make money / hire well / solve a work problem." *LinkedIn example:* a message to a hiring manager showing you'd solve exactly the problem their team posted about.
- **Social** — "We're connected / from the same world / would enjoy knowing each other." *LinkedIn example:* a shared school, a mutual connection, the same community or interest.
- **Emotional** — "I admire your work / want to support what you do / expect nothing back." *LinkedIn example:* a specific, genuine comment on someone's post, or sharing their work with your own audience.

If you can't name the value, you're not ready to send — research until you find the true angle.

## 2. Target mapping — reachable vs. reach

Build every outreach list in **two tiers**:

- **Reachable tier** — people you can plausibly get a response from: peers and recent alumni, colleagues, people in your community, analysts/individual contributors at target companies, active posters in your field. These are where replies and momentum come from.
- **Reach tier** — the people you actually want: the hiring manager, the director, the founder, the person whose job you'd love. You rarely land these cold. You reach them *through* the reachable tier and warm introductions.

**How to build the list:**
1. Start from the goal (a specific role, a company, a field, an audience).
2. List 5–10 reach-tier targets tied directly to that goal.
3. For each reach target, list the reachable people around them (their team's ICs, shared alumni, mutual connections).
4. Prioritize reachable people who are *active* on LinkedIn (they post/comment) — they're far likelier to respond.

**Quantity mindset:** cold outreach is a numbers game with a skill curve. Expect most messages to be ignored — that's normal and not personal. A batch of ~10 well-researched, personalized messages typically returns 2–3 replies. Your first messages will be your weakest; volume plus iteration fixes that. Send in small, personal batches — never bulk-blast.

## 3. The research checklist

Two minutes of research is the difference between a reply and a swipe. For each target, pull from their profile and activity:

- **Current role and company** — what they do now, seniority, team.
- **Recent activity** — their last few posts, comments, reshares, or articles. *This is the richest source of angles.*
- **Career path** — where they came from (shared school? shared past employer? a jump you admire?).
- **Mutual connections** — anyone who could introduce you, or who you can honestly reference.
- **Stated interests** — what they say they care about (in their about section, posts, or featured items).
- **Company news** — a recent launch, funding, hire, or challenge you could speak to.

From this, write down **1–2 concrete angles** before drafting anything. An angle is a specific, true hook — "commented that dashboard refresh time is their biggest pain," not "works in data."

When a connected tool or web search is available, use it to pull this *public* info so the angle is current and accurate. Prefer looking it up over guessing.

## 4. Cadence and sequence (timing)

A typical warm, non-spammy sequence for one target:

1. **Day 0 — Warm up (optional but effective):** leave a thoughtful comment on a recent post so your name is familiar.
2. **Day 1–2 — Connection request** with a personalized ≤300-char note (or InMail if not connectable).
3. **On accept — Opening DM:** thank them, add value, make a small specific ask (or no ask yet, for brand/give-first).
4. **+5–7 days if no reply — First follow-up:** add a small update or useful thing; restate the light ask.
5. **+5–7 days if still no reply — Second follow-up:** graceful close, leave the door open, then stop.
6. **After any real conversation — Thank-you within 24h**, then the ask for a warm intro if it fits.
7. **Ongoing — Nurture touchpoints** every few months for relationships worth keeping.

For a **campaign** (e.g., a two-week job-search push): pick ~10–15 targets across both tiers, stagger the Day-0/Day-1 steps across the first several days so follow-ups don't all bunch up, and track each person's stage (see the tracker in section 10).

## 5. The warm-introduction engine

Warm intros beat cold outreach every time, and they're the mechanism behind compounding. The engine:

1. Cold-outreach the reachable tier (accept that half of conversations may be dead ends).
2. In the ones that click, end with the ask: *"Is there anyone else you'd be open to introducing me to?"*
3. When reaching the reach-tier target, arrive warm: *"I've been speaking with {A} and {B} on your team, and they suggested I reach out to you."* You're no longer a stranger.
4. Make every intro **easy to make** — hand the connector a two-line forwardable blurb so saying yes costs them nothing.

Each good conversation should aim to produce one or two more. That's the flywheel: contacts → conversations → intros → more contacts. It only spins if you also nurture (section 6) and build your own value (section 7).

## 6. The nurturing system (trust + value)

Getting in the door is step one, not the finish. "Play long-term games with long-term people." Relationships that pay dividends are *nurtured*, two ways:

**Build trust** — the three pillars:
- **Reliability** — do what you say you'll do, every single time.
- **Consistency** — show up regularly, not just when you need something.
- **Authenticity** — never salesy, never scummy. People can always tell.

**Deliver value** — practical moves:
- **Ask about their goals.** "What are your top goals right now?" Then think about how you can genuinely help. People always make time for someone who adds to their world.
- **Send genuine touchpoints.** A congrats on a milestone, a relevant article ("saw this and thought of you"), an honest check-in. (Templates in `message-templates.md` §7.)
- **Write thoughtful thank-you notes.** Name the specific thing you valued and what you'll do with it.
- **Become a connector.** Introducing the right people to each other is high-value and builds your reputation fast.

**Give without keeping score.** Do these things with no expectation of return. People sense genuine generosity, and over time it compounds into a reputation others actively seek out — that's the karma flywheel.

---

## 7. Building your own value

Your value should scale with your network. The most powerful position is being the person *others* want to reach. Build credibility in parallel with outreach:

- **Career wins** — real results and projects you can point to.
- **A personal brand** — post consistently in your field; implied credibility comes from a visible body of work. It starts at zero and gets less cringe-y over time; the only requirement is to begin.
- **An audience** — even a small, engaged following makes senior people and creators open to you, because you can offer reach and social value.
- **Taking a real swing** — starting something (a project, a product, an initiative) makes people want to talk to you.

The key mindset: the two things that build credibility — publishing your work and starting things — **need no one's permission.** You just start, expect it to take a while to pay off, and let it compound.

## 8. Tone calibration by goal

Match the voice to the goal in play:

- **Job search** — respectful, curious, low-pressure. Lead with genuine interest in their work; make the ask a small amount of insight or being "on the radar" for relevant roles, not "give me a job." Always offer an easy out.
- **General / peer networking** — mutual and equal. Frame it as two people in the same world worth knowing; offer as much as you ask.
- **Brand / audience growth** — give-first, **no ask at all** early. Comment, share their work, be generous and specific. The relationship, and any later opportunity, grows from the value you added.

Across all three: warm, specific, concise, human. Never desperate, never robotic, never a wall of text.

---

## 9. Anti-patterns to avoid

- **Walls of text.** If it doesn't scan on a phone, it won't be read.
- **Generic subject lines / openers.** "Informational interview request" or "I hope this finds you well" get swiped. Open with a specific hook.
- **"I want your job." / "Can you get me a referral?"** as a first message. Too much, too soon.
- **Copy-paste that could go to anyone.** No true, specific detail = no reply.
- **Two or more asks in one message.** Pick the one that matters.
- **Endless follow-ups.** One or two gracious nudges, then stop.
- **Only reaching out when you need something.** That's extraction, not networking, and people feel it.
- **Bulk automation / scraping to auto-send.** Risks your account and reads as spam. Draft, personalize, send in small batches yourself.

## 10. The outreach tracker (copy-paste)

Keep one row per target. This makes a campaign manageable and keeps follow-ups from slipping.

```
| Name | Tier (Reachable/Reach) | Company | Angle (specific hook) | Value type | Date reached | Stage | Next step | Notes |
|------|------------------------|---------|-----------------------|------------|--------------|-------|-----------|-------|
|      |                        |         |                       |            |              |       |           |       |
```

**Stage values:** `Not started` → `Warmed up (commented)` → `Request sent` → `Connected` → `Conversation booked` → `Talked` → `Intro requested` → `Nurturing`.

Fill `Angle` before you send — if you can't, go back to research (section 3). Review the tracker every few days during an active campaign, and move each person one stage forward where you can.
