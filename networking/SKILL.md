# Networking (LinkedIn)

A value-first playbook for building a professional network on LinkedIn: from first cold outreach, to warm introductions, to long-term relationships that compound over time. It serves three overlapping goals — landing a role (job search), general professional networking, and growing a personal brand or audience — and adapts its tone and call-to-action to whichever one is in play.

## The one idea everything hangs on: lead with value

The person you want to reach is busy and ruthlessly filters their inbox. Every generic message gets swiped away. To earn a reply you have to cross the "I'll pay attention to you" threshold, and the reliable way to do that is to make it obvious you offer them some kind of **value**:

- **Financial value** — you could help them make money, do business, hire well, or solve a work problem.
- **Social value** — a shared connection, community, school, or interest; someone they'd want in their orbit.
- **Emotional value** — genuine admiration, encouragement, giving back, or paying it forward with nothing expected in return.

Before drafting anything, answer one question: *what value does this specific person care about, and what's my honest angle into it?* If there's no angle yet, the first job is research (Mode A), not writing.

## The end-to-end loop

1. **Pick targets — two tiers.** *Reachable*: colleagues, classmates, recent alumni, people in your community, peers/analysts at target companies. *Reach*: the hiring manager, the director, the person you actually want. You rarely land the reach target cold — you get there by connecting with reachable people first, then earning a warm intro. Always build the list with both tiers.

2. **Research.** Know their role, recent activity/posts, career path, mutual connections, stated interests, and their company's recent news. This is where the angle comes from. Details and a checklist live in `references/playbook.md`.

3. **Outreach.** Send a personalized message that leads with value and is short enough to read on a phone. On LinkedIn this is usually a connection request with a note (≤300 characters), sometimes a warm comment first, sometimes an InMail. Templates and length rules in `references/message-templates.md`. Expect most cold outreach to be ignored — that's normal. Sending 10 good, tailored messages typically yields 2–3 replies.

4. **Conversation.** Open with light rapport (small talk is just tuning into each other's energy, not really about the weather), then show you came prepared: ask a specific question about a real thing they did ("I saw you led the migration to X — how did that go?"), not a generic "what's your job like?" End a good conversation with a **call to action**: *"I really enjoyed this — is there anyone else you'd be open to introducing me to?"*

5. **Warm intros → compounding.** Warm introductions beat cold outreach every time. Each good conversation can produce one or two more, which produce more. This is the flywheel — but it only spins if you also do steps 6 and 7.

6. **Nurture the right relationships (play the long game).** Getting in the door is step one, not the finish. Deepen relationships two ways: **build trust** (reliability — do what you say, every time; consistency — show up regularly; authenticity — never salesy or scummy) and **deliver value** (ask "what are your top goals?" and help; send genuine touchpoints — a congrats, a relevant article, a real check-in; write thoughtful thank-you notes). Give without keeping score; people sense it, and it compounds into a reputation others seek out. See `references/playbook.md` (Nurturing).

7. **Build your own value.** Your value should scale with your network. Grow your credibility in parallel — career wins, a personal brand, an audience (e.g. posting consistently). You don't need anyone's permission to start; you just start, and let it compound. Elite people engage with you when you add to *their* world too.

## LinkedIn specifics (quick reference)

- **Connection request note: 300 characters max.** Every character counts — one sharp, personalized line beats a paragraph you can't fit anyway.
- **Warm up first when you can.** A thoughtful comment on a recent post before you send the request makes the name familiar and lifts accept rates.
- **Connection note vs. DM vs. InMail.** Note = the short hook attached to a request. DM = the real message once connected (more room, still concise). InMail = paid, reaches people you're not connected to; make the first line earn the open.
- **Optimize for mobile scannability.** No walls of text. Short sentences. One clear ask.
- **Personalize or don't send.** A message that could be copy-pasted to 100 people reads like it was. Reference something true and specific to them.

## Output quality bar

Everything this skill produces should be:

- **Personalized** to a real detail about the person (a post, a project, a shared thing, their company's news). Never generic.
- **Punchy and concise** — respect the reader's time and the platform's limits.
- **Human and authentic**, never salesy, robotic, or desperate. Warm, specific, confident.
- **Value-led** — the "why you'd care" is clear early, and the ask (if any) is light and specific.
- **Optioned when useful** — for important messages, offer 2 variants with different angles (e.g. a shared-connection angle vs. an admired-their-work angle) and note the tradeoff, so the user can pick what fits.

Adapt tone to the goal: **job search** leans respectful and curious with a soft ask for insight/referral; **general networking** leans peer-to-peer and mutual; **brand/audience** leans give-first with no ask at all early on. Details in `references/playbook.md` (Tone).

## Working with the user's LinkedIn tools

Default to **drafting and coaching** — produce the messages, plans, and sequences, and let the user review and send them personally. This keeps them in control and protects their account: LinkedIn restricts automation, and bulk auto-connecting or auto-messaging through scrapers/bots risks getting an account limited or banned. Do **not** auto-send outreach or connection requests; sending stays a human decision the user makes.

Where it genuinely helps personalization, it's fine to use web search or an available connected tool to look up a target's *public* information (current role, recent posts, mutual connections, company news) so the angle is specific and current. Prefer this over guessing. If the user asks to bulk-scrape or automate sending, explain the account-safety and terms-of-service risk and offer the safer path: research-assisted drafting that they send in small, personal batches.

## A quick before/after (the standard to hit)

**Weak (generic, gets ignored):**
> Subject: Informational interview request
> Hi, I'd love to learn about your job and get any advice on breaking into data analytics. Thanks!

**Strong (specific, value-led, fits in a 300-char note):**
> Hi Priya — your post on cutting the dashboard refresh time with incremental Power BI models was genuinely useful; I rebuilt one of my project pipelines the same way. I'm an early-career analyst in Bangalore and would love to connect and learn from your work.

The difference is research plus a true, specific hook — not length. Hold every draft to that standard.
